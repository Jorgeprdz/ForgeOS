# Forge Readiness Signals Discovery

Status: DISCOVERY

No implementation. No code. No engines. No schemas. No UI. No Build Tree
changes. No architecture modifications. No readiness scores. No readiness
labels.

## 1. Executive Summary

Readiness signals are observable evidence patterns suggesting that a prospect is
moving from awareness toward action.

They are not proof.

They must be interpreted in context, especially because politeness, curiosity
and agreement can imitate readiness.

## 2. Readiness Signal Families

### 1. Need Ownership Signals

Evidence:

- prospect describes the need in their own words
- prospect names who or what would be affected
- prospect identifies a consequence of doing nothing
- prospect ranks one need as more important than others
- prospect stops treating the problem as generic

Interpretation:

The prospect may have moved from awareness to ownership.

### 2. Trust and Disclosure Signals

Evidence:

- prospect answers openly
- prospect shares financial or family context
- prospect shares concerns, fears or priorities
- prospect corrects incomplete advisor assumptions
- prospect permits deeper discovery

Interpretation:

The prospect may trust the advisor enough to make the conversation real.

### 3. Commitment Signals

Evidence:

- prospect discusses monetary capacity
- prospect offers a realistic budget range
- prospect agrees to a closing appointment
- prospect includes necessary decision-makers
- prospect agrees to gather documents or information
- prospect accepts the next step as specific, not vague

Interpretation:

The prospect may be moving from interest to commitment.

### 4. Implementation Question Signals

Evidence:

- asks what the next step would be
- asks when coverage or process could begin
- asks what information will be needed
- asks whether spouse or partner should attend
- asks how payment or underwriting works
- asks what happens after applying

Interpretation:

The prospect may be thinking beyond the recommendation into execution.

### 5. Option Comparison Signals

Evidence:

- compares options instead of debating the need
- asks tradeoff questions
- asks affordability questions
- asks "what would you recommend based on what I told you?"
- asks what changes if budget is higher or lower

Interpretation:

The prospect may have accepted the problem and moved into solution selection.

### 6. Clarification Instead of Justification Signals

Evidence:

- asks for clarity, not persuasion
- asks how a mechanism works
- asks whether the recommendation matches the stated priority
- asks what is included or excluded
- asks what could go wrong

Interpretation:

The prospect may not be resisting the need; they may be validating fit.

### 7. Referral or Advocacy Signals

Evidence:

- gives referrals after a valuable first meeting
- says someone else should hear the same explanation
- introduces another decision-maker
- says the conversation helped them think differently

Interpretation:

The prospect may already perceive value before product presentation.

## 3. Strongest Readiness Combinations

The strongest evidence appears when these signals cluster:

- need ownership
- open disclosure
- monetary commitment
- decision-maker involvement
- specific next appointment
- implementation questions

One signal alone is fragile.

A coherent pattern is stronger.

## 4. What Readiness Signals Are Not

Readiness signals are not:

- a score
- a label
- guaranteed close probability
- permission to pressure
- proof of suitability
- proof of underwriting acceptance
- proof of policy placement

They are evidence patterns.

## 5. Practical Discovery Conclusion

The prospect who has already decided usually stops debating whether the problem
is real and starts exploring how action would work.
