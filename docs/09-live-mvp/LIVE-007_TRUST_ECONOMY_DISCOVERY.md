# LIVE-007_TRUST_ECONOMY_DISCOVERY

## 1. Executive Summary

The **Trust Economy** is the framework through which Forge OS understands and measures the reliability of interactions within its ecosystem. Rather than a "Social Score," trust is treated as **Relational Capital**—a resource that facilitates smoother interactions, higher adoption of recommendations, and deeper client relationships.

**Final Principle**: Trust is relational capital. Forge should preserve it, not exploit it.

---

## 2. Core Principles

*   **Trust is Earned**: Reliability is demonstrated through consistent behavior over time.
*   **Trust Decays**: Past performance is not a permanent guarantee; trust requires continuous maintenance.
*   **Trust is Contextual**: An advisor may be highly trusted in "Client Relationships" but have low trust in "Administrative Accuracy."
*   **Trust is Non-Manipulable**: Trust cannot be "bought" or directly edited; it is a derived byproduct of actions and outcomes.

---

## 3. Trust Domains

Forge models trust across six key actors:

| Domain | Description | High Trust Indicator |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Advisor** | Reliability of the advisor to the system and manager. | Consistent follow-up on high-priority alerts. |
| **Manager** | Reliability of coaching and leadership commitments. | Commitments made in coaching sessions are honored. |
| **Client** | Reliability of the client to fulfill financial obligations. | Consistent payment history and responsiveness. |
| **Referrer** | Quality and conversion rate of provided leads. | High percentage of referrals reaching "Cita" status. |
| **NASH** | System's reliability to the advisor (Decision Accuracy). | High "Action-to-Outcome" rate for suggestions. |
| **Carrier** | Reliability of the insurer (Product and Process). | Stable pricing and efficient claims/processing. |

---

## 4. Sources of Trust (Accumulation)

Trust builds when expectations are met or exceeded:
*   **Commitments Honored**: Meeting a deadline set in the CRM.
*   **Follow-ups Completed**: Responding to alerts within their critical window.
*   **Successful Recommendations**: NASH earns trust when its prompts lead to positive outcomes.
*   **Transparency**: Explaining *why* a decision was made.
*   **Ethical Behavior**: Compliance with carrier and regulatory guardrails.

---

## 5. Trust Erosion (Depletion)

Trust is lost when expectations are failed:
*   **Broken Commitments**: Missing a scheduled meeting or task.
*   **Excessive Overrides**: Frequently dismissing accurate NASH prompts suggests a mismatch.
*   **Low Outcome Rates**: If actions lead to failure, the actor's reliability score drops.
*   **Ignored Recommendations**: Silent ignore is more erosive than explicit rejection.
*   **Data Neglect**: Allowing a pipeline or portfolio to become stale.

---

## 6. Safeguards & Ethics

Forge must adhere to strict ethical boundaries to prevent the Trust Economy from becoming a punitive social score.

*   **No Human Worth Ranking**: Forge must never rank one human as "better" than another based on trust scores.
*   **No Hidden Scores**: Every trust-related metric must be viewable and explainable to the human it describes.
*   **No Auto-Punishment**: Low trust scores must trigger **Support** (e.g., more coaching), not **Sanctions** (e.g., losing access to features).
*   **Auditability**: Every change in trust status must be linked to a specific Evidence and Outcome record.

---

## 7. Risks & Open Questions

### 7.1 Risks
*   **Gamification**: Actors performing "fake" tasks just to boost trust metrics.
*   **Feedback Loops**: A low trust score leading to fewer opportunities, which prevents trust from being re-earned.
*   **Algorithmic Bias**: Unfairly penalizing advisors for factors outside their control (e.g., a difficult market).

### 7.2 Open Questions
*   How should trust "Reset" after a long period of inactivity?
*   Should trust be shared? (e.g., "Agency Trust" vs. "Advisor Trust").
