# Forge Friction Intelligence Discovery

Status: DISCOVERY

No implementation. No code. No engines. No schemas. No UI. No Build Tree
changes. No architecture modifications. No friction scores. No rankings. No
labels.

## 1. Executive Summary

Friction is the force that prevents action even when need, awareness,
consequence and recommendation may already exist.

The core discovery:

People often fail to act not because they lack motivation, but because they have
competing motivations, unresolved constraints, fear, ambiguity, relational
dependency, cognitive load or environmental blocks.

Friction is not the same as objection.

An objection is usually spoken.

Friction may be silent.

## 2. Core Question

Why do people fail to act even when they believe action would benefit them?

Because "belief that action is beneficial" is not the same as "capacity to move
now."

Action requires:

- recognized need
- trust
- ownership
- clarity
- emotional safety
- economic possibility
- decision-maker alignment
- next-step simplicity
- behavioral follow-through
- timing capacity

If any of these is blocked, action can stall even after agreement.

## 3. Definitions

### Objection

A stated reason for not proceeding, usually surfaced during appointment,
presentation, close or follow-up.

Example:

"It is too expensive."

Objection may be the real issue, or it may be a socially acceptable expression
of a deeper friction.

### Concern

A legitimate worry, uncertainty or risk perception that the person can usually
name.

Example:

"I am not sure if this fits my cash flow."

Concern can be resolved through clarity, evidence, adjustment or time.

### Friction

The internal or external force that makes action harder than agreement.

Example:

"I know I should do this, but I keep delaying because I am afraid of making the
wrong decision."

Friction can exist even when the person agrees with the recommendation.

### Barrier

A block that prevents action unless removed or changed.

Example:

Missing documents, absent spouse, no available payment method, underwriting
issue or no real economic capacity.

### Constraint

A real limitation that shapes what action is possible.

Example:

Budget, time, health, legal status, decision authority or documentation.

Constraint is not resistance. It is reality.

### Resistance

Active or passive pushback against the process, advisor, recommendation,
timing, commitment or emotional implication.

Example:

Avoiding follow-up after agreeing the need exists.

### Inaction

The outcome state where no meaningful next step happens.

Inaction can be caused by objection, concern, friction, barrier, constraint,
resistance or weak readiness.

## 4. Are They the Same?

No.

They overlap, but they are different phenomena.

Objection is language.

Concern is worry.

Friction is action drag.

Barrier is blockage.

Constraint is limitation.

Resistance is pushback.

Inaction is the result.

## 5. Evidence Sources

Pedro Camarena Intelligence:

Risk recognition can create action pressure, but the prospect may still need to
choose which risk to administer. Friction can appear when risk is recognized but
the person avoids prioritization.

SMNYL Methodology:

The manual anticipates "no tengo tiempo," "no tengo dinero," "ya cuento con un
asesor," "no estoy interesado" and "mandamelo por correo." These are treated as
objections, but they also reveal time, money, trust, attention and avoidance
frictions.

Garry Kinder Methodology:

Kinder separates need recognition, monetary commitment, presentation and close.
It also emphasizes that confused prospects rarely buy, that spouses and
decision-makers matter, and that advisor fear of rejection creates resistance to
action.

Existing Forge Readiness Discovery:

Readiness can exist before action, and false readiness can imitate commitment.
This discovery adds the missing layer: even true readiness can coexist with
friction.

## 6. Need Exists, Decision Exists, Action Does Not

Examples:

1. The prospect recognizes the risk but has not resolved how much money can be
   responsibly allocated.

Blocking factor:

Financial constraint or affordability uncertainty.

2. The prospect wants to proceed but spouse or business partner is not present.

Blocking factor:

Relational decision dependency.

3. The prospect understands the recommendation but delays documents.

Blocking factor:

Behavioral friction, administrative friction or emotional avoidance.

4. The prospect says the recommendation makes sense but wants to keep
   researching.

Blocking factor:

Cognitive uncertainty, fear of regret or need for decision confidence.

5. The advisor knows a follow-up is needed but avoids the call.

Blocking factor:

Advisor-side fear of rejection or conflict.

## 7. Where Friction Appears in the Readiness Sequence

Awareness:

Friction may appear as low attention, busyness or topic avoidance.

Recognition:

Friction may appear as minimization: "Yes, but not for me."

Ownership:

Friction may appear as emotional discomfort or fear of consequence.

Commitment:

Friction may appear as vague budget, missing decision-maker or delayed next
step.

Decision:

Friction may appear as fear of regret, option paralysis or hidden doubt.

Action:

Friction may appear as missing documents, payment delay, underwriting avoidance
or failure to complete implementation.

## 8. Is Friction Lack of Motivation or Competing Motivation?

Usually, friction is a competing motivation.

Examples:

- desire to protect family competes with fear of losing cash flow
- desire to decide competes with fear of regret
- desire to trust advisor competes with fear of being pressured
- desire to act competes with desire to preserve options
- desire to coach competes with manager conflict avoidance
- desire to prospect competes with advisor fear of rejection

Friction is not always laziness or weak motivation. Often, it is another motive
pulling in the opposite direction.

## 9. Discovery Conclusion

Friction Intelligence should understand why action stalls without assuming the
person lacks need, motivation or awareness.

The most precise answer to the final question is:

If a person says, "I know I should do this," but still does not act, what is
most likely missing?

Most likely missing:

Resolved action safety.

The person may need one or more of:

- emotional safety
- decision confidence
- economic clarity
- relational alignment
- reduced complexity
- concrete next step
- removed barrier
- trust that action will not create regret
