## 1. Executive Summary

This discovery investigates the working hypothesis:

"The transaction may occur in the second meeting. The decision may occur in the
first meeting."

The hypothesis is supported, but only with careful wording.

The evidence suggests that many successful sales are psychologically advanced
before the product presentation. In those cases, the first meeting creates
readiness through trust, need recognition, ownership and monetary commitment.
The second meeting may then function as recommendation validation, closing and
administrative execution.

However, the hypothesis is not universally true. The presentation can still
create or destroy the sale when:

- the need was not fully owned
- the monetary commitment was vague
- the recommendation is unclear
- the prospect does not understand the solution
- a spouse, partner or decision-maker was absent
- price, trust or suitability concerns emerge

Discovery verdict:

The decision and transaction are often separate events. The presentation does
not always create the sale, but it often reveals whether readiness was already
formed.

## 3. Attempt to Prove the Hypothesis

Evidence supporting the hypothesis:

### Pedro Camarena Intelligence

Pedro's risk script creates the key movement before product presentation:

From:

"I am exposed to risks."

To:

"I should administer or transfer a risk that already exists."

The internal decision begins when the prospect recognizes a personal risk and
chooses which risk should be administered.

### SMNYL Methodology

The Manual de Induccion separates first and second meetings.

First meeting includes:

- purpose, process and benefit
- priority establishment
- ANF
- agreement on detected needs
- monetary commitment
- qualified referrals
- next appointment

Second meeting includes:

- solution presentation
- benefits
- objections
- review of detected needs agreement
- confirm monetary commitment
- close

This strongly supports the idea that readiness is expected before the formal
solution presentation.

### Garry Kinder Methodology

Kinder defines a prospect qualified after a first meeting when the prospect:

- recognizes a need
- has payment capacity and monetary commitment
- answers openly and with trust
- is insurable
- will buy from the advisor because trust and mutual liking exist

Kinder also places monetary commitment before recommendation preparation.

That means the second meeting is not meant to discover whether the prospect has
a need. It is meant to validate and close a recommendation built from the first
meeting.

## 4. Attempt to Disprove the Hypothesis

Evidence against overclaiming the hypothesis:

### Presentation Still Matters

Kinder states that the presentation must be simple, clear and professional. A
confused prospect rarely buys. This means the presentation can still create or
destroy action.

### First Meeting Readiness Can Be False

The prospect may appear interested but not own the need. Politeness, curiosity
or agreement can be mistaken for decision.

### Commitment Can Be Weak

If the monetary commitment is vague, reluctant or advisor-suggested rather than
owned by the prospect, the decision may not exist yet.

### Missing Decision-Makers Can Delay Real Decision

If a spouse, partner or business decision-maker is absent, the first meeting may
produce individual readiness but not household or business readiness.

### Product Fit Can Change the Decision

A prospect may recognize a risk but reject a specific recommendation if it feels
misaligned, unaffordable, too complex or insufficiently explained.

## 5. Core Discovery

The first meeting can create decision readiness.

The second meeting can convert readiness into action.

But readiness is not proof of transaction.

Readiness is an evidence pattern showing that the prospect has moved from vague
concern to owned commitment.

## 6. What Observable Evidence Indicates Decision Before Presentation?

Possible evidence:

- prospect names the need in their own words
- prospect agrees the need is personally important
- prospect identifies consequence of inaction
- prospect discloses facts and feelings openly
- prospect discusses budget or money realistically
- prospect asks what the next step would look like
- prospect asks timing or implementation questions
- prospect involves spouse, partner or decision-maker
- prospect compares options instead of debating whether the need exists
- prospect confirms a next meeting for solution review
- prospect gives referrals because the conversation already had value
- prospect asks clarification questions, not justification questions

None of these is proof alone. They become stronger when they appear together.

## 7. Final Question

If a skilled advisor walks out of the first meeting and says:

"The sale is already done,"

the statement is justified only if evidence shows:

- trust exists
- the prospect recognized and owned the need
- the prospect connected the need to personal consequence
- the prospect disclosed meaningful facts and feelings
- the prospect made a realistic monetary commitment
- the decision-maker context is complete or intentionally managed
- the next meeting is framed as solution validation, not need persuasion
- the prospect's questions shifted from "Do I need this?" to "How would we do
  this?"

The statement is wrong if:

- the prospect was merely polite
- the prospect did not own the need
- the prospect avoided money
- the decision-maker was missing
- the next appointment is vague
- the prospect still debates the existence or relevance of the risk
- the advisor is projecting confidence onto weak evidence

## 8. Discovery Verdict

Verdict:

SUPPORTED AS A READINESS HYPOTHESIS, NOT AS A UNIVERSAL RULE

Reason:

The evidence supports separating psychological decision formation from formal
transaction execution. But no source proves that every sale is decided before
presentation. The better principle is:

Many sales are won or lost before the presentation, but the presentation remains
the moment where readiness is validated, clarified and converted into action.
