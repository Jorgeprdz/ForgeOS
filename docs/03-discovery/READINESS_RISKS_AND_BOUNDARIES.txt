READINESS RISKS AND BOUNDARIES

Status:
ARCHITECTURE DISCOVERY

Scope:
No implementation. No code. No UI. No schemas. No engines. No scores. No
rankings. No Build Tree changes.

CORE RISK

Readiness can be misused to justify promotion, delegation, exclusion,
surveillance or pressure.

Readiness must be evidence-based, responsibility-specific and development
oriented.

BOUNDARY 1: READINESS IS RESPONSIBILITY-SPECIFIC

Risk:
"Ready" becomes a broad label.

Boundary:
Always ask "ready for what?"

BOUNDARY 2: READINESS IS NOT PRODUCTION

Risk:
High producers receive responsibility they cannot handle.

Boundary:
Production is evidence, not proof.

BOUNDARY 3: READINESS IS NOT TENURE

Risk:
Time is mistaken for growth.

Boundary:
Tenure must be interpreted through capability and behavior.

BOUNDARY 4: READINESS IS NOT CONFIDENCE

Risk:
Confident people receive responsibility before competence exists.

Boundary:
Confidence needs evidence.

BOUNDARY 5: READINESS IS NOT INFORMATION

Risk:
Training completion or certification is treated as proof.

Boundary:
Information supports readiness only when converted into responsible action.

BOUNDARY 6: READINESS IS NOT MANAGER PREFERENCE

Risk:
Bias, favoritism or urgency drives promotion.

Boundary:
Manager input is context or evidence, not truth by itself.

BOUNDARY 7: READINESS IS NOT EXCELLENCE

Risk:
Excellent current performers are promoted into responsibilities they are not
ready for.

Boundary:
Excellence in one responsibility does not prove readiness for another.

BOUNDARY 8: READINESS IS NOT POTENTIAL

Risk:
Potential becomes premature responsibility.

Boundary:
Potential should lead to development until readiness evidence exists.

BOUNDARY 9: READINESS IS NOT SURVEILLANCE

Risk:
Managers use readiness signals to monitor and pressure.

Boundary:
Readiness should support trust, delegation, coaching and development.

BOUNDARY 10: NOT READY IS NOT FAILURE

Risk:
People are stigmatized.

Boundary:
Not ready should produce a development path.

RISK SCENARIOS

Scenario 1:
High-producing advisor is promoted to manager without coaching evidence.

Risk:
Production without people-development capability.

Correct handling:
Mark strong production, but require leadership readiness evidence.

Scenario 2:
Long-tenured advisor receives autonomy despite weak follow-through.

Risk:
Tenure without readiness.

Correct handling:
Treat tenure as context; require accountability evidence.

Scenario 3:
Candidate is charismatic and confident.

Risk:
Interview performance mistaken for readiness.

Correct handling:
Treat charisma as weak evidence; require behavior and follow-through signals.

Scenario 4:
Manager wants to promote a preferred person.

Risk:
Manager bias.

Correct handling:
Require evidence, confidence and human review if signals conflict.

Scenario 5:
Person is capable but never given opportunity.

Risk:
Capability without responsibility stalls development.

Correct handling:
Recommend supported stretch responsibility.

Scenario 6:
Person is not ready but receives responsibility anyway.

Risk:
Avoidable failure, client risk, team harm.

Correct handling:
Recommend development path before assignment.

PREMATURE PROMOTION RISKS

- responsibility without capability
- client harm
- team instability
- advisor burnout
- false confidence
- trust damage
- manager credibility loss
- avoidable regression

PROMOTION TOO LATE RISKS

- stagnation
- lost talent
- reduced confidence
- resentment
- missed leverage
- underdeveloped leadership pipeline
- capability without opportunity

FALSE READINESS CAUSES

- survivorship bias
- recency bias
- charisma bias
- manager preference
- production-only thinking
- tenure assumptions
- confidence bias
- activity volume bias
- certification bias
- lack of negative evidence

CONSTITUTIONAL BOUNDARIES TO CONSIDER

1. Readiness requires evidence.
2. Readiness is responsibility-specific.
3. Readiness is not a score or ranking.
4. Not ready is not failure.
5. Potential, Readiness and Excellence are distinct.
6. Production, tenure, confidence and training do not prove readiness alone.
7. Manager input must not override evidence.
8. Readiness signals must not become surveillance.
9. Readiness should produce development paths.
10. Greater responsibility requires explicit context and risk review.

FINAL RISK VERDICT

Readiness is constitutionally valuable only if Forge treats it as an
evidence-supported responsibility judgment, not a broad label.

Verdict:
CANDIDATE FOR LOCK.
