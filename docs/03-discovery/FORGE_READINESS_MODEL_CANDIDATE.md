# Forge Readiness Model Candidate

Status: DISCOVERY

No implementation. No code. No engines. No schemas. No UI. No Build Tree
changes. No architecture modifications. No readiness scores. No readiness
labels.

## 1. Executive Summary

This model candidate describes how a prospect may become ready to decide.

It is not a score, rating, engine, schema or architecture change.

It is an evidence-pattern model for future review.

## 2. Candidate Readiness Sequence

Awareness
-> Recognition
-> Ownership
-> Commitment
-> Decision
-> Action

## 3. Stage Definitions

### Awareness

Definition:

The prospect becomes aware that a topic, risk or goal exists.

Observable markers:

- listens
- asks initial questions
- acknowledges the topic
- accepts that the conversation may be relevant

Not enough:

Awareness alone is not readiness.

### Recognition

Definition:

The prospect sees that the topic may apply to their life.

Observable markers:

- connects the topic to personal context
- identifies an existing risk or goal
- reacts to consequence
- acknowledges a gap

Not enough:

Recognition can still be intellectual, not owned.

### Ownership

Definition:

The prospect accepts that the need or risk is personally important.

Observable markers:

- describes the need in their own words
- identifies who is affected
- prioritizes one need over others
- expresses concern, desire or responsibility

Not enough:

Ownership without capacity or next step may remain emotional concern.

### Commitment

Definition:

The prospect expresses willingness to allocate resources toward action.

Observable markers:

- monetary commitment
- next appointment
- decision-maker involvement
- document or information gathering
- agreement to review a recommendation

Not enough:

Commitment can still be tentative.

### Decision

Definition:

The prospect internally concludes that action should be taken if the
recommendation fits.

Observable markers:

- asks implementation questions
- compares options
- validates fit
- shifts from "why" to "how"
- treats the second meeting as solution confirmation

Not enough:

Decision still needs recommendation clarity and action.

### Action

Definition:

The prospect proceeds with the chosen course.

Observable markers:

- accepts recommendation
- signs / submits application where applicable
- pays or begins payment process
- completes required steps
- participates in implementation

Not enough:

Action can still fail before placement if doubts, underwriting, payment or
delivery issues appear.

## 4. Relationship Between Concepts

Need Recognition:

The prospect sees the need.

Trust:

The prospect allows the advisor into the decision.

Commitment:

The prospect allocates resources toward solving it.

Decision:

The prospect concludes action should happen.

Recommendation:

The advisor proposes the specific path.

Action:

The prospect moves from intent to execution.

## 5. Support and Refutation of Presentation Statement

Statement:

"The presentation does not create the sale. The presentation reveals whether the
sale already exists."

Support:

- SMNYL first meeting captures detected needs and monetary commitment before
  solution presentation.
- Kinder requires need recognition and monetary commitment before the
  recommendation.
- Pedro's script can create risk-transfer desire before product details.

Refutation:

- Kinder emphasizes that confusing presentations prevent buying.
- The recommendation may create new understanding.
- Product fit, price and clarity can change the decision.
- Missing decision-makers can keep the sale undecided.
- A presentation can expose that first-meeting readiness was false.

Balanced candidate:

The presentation does not always create the sale. In strong cases, it confirms
and converts readiness already formed. In weak cases, it must still create
understanding, resolve doubt and test whether readiness exists.

## 6. Final Candidate

Readiness should be treated as a pattern of evidence that a prospect has moved
from awareness to owned commitment and is prepared to evaluate a specific
recommendation.

It must not be treated as:

- a label
- a score
- a probability
- a pressure trigger
- a replacement for advisor judgment
